## The AI Safety Connection

This project is AI safety work, even though it doesn't look like traditional alignment research.

### The Core Safety Question

As AI becomes capable of generating creative content, we face a choice:
1. **Human creativity becomes economically irrelevant** â€” AI generates everything, humans are displaced
2. **Human judgment remains the copyrightable/valuable act** â€” AI assists, but human selection and curation is what matters

This project forces legal systems to make that determination *now*, while the question is still open.

### Why Early Precedent Matters

If we wait until AI generation is ubiquitous, corporate interests will have already shaped the legal landscape. By creating a test case now, we:
- Establish that human curation CAN be the copyrightable act (if we win)
- Or establish that algorithmically-assisted content can't be monopolized (if we lose)

Either precedent constrains future AI-driven content monopolies.

### The Deeper Question

What makes a creative work *human*?

- Is it the physical act of playing notes? (Then MIDI programming isn't music)
- Is it the conception of the melody? (Then humming into a transcription app counts)
- Is it the judgment that THIS melody is good and THAT one isn't? (Then curation is creation)

This project argues for the third position: **human creative agency lives in judgment, selection, and arrangement â€” not in mechanical execution**. 

That's not just a copyright argument. It's an argument about what humans are for in an AI world.

## The Legal Theory

### What We're Claiming

Individual 3-note motifs are **scÃ¨nes Ã  faire** â€” stock musical phrases that any tonal composer must use. They are not copyrightable.

Individual rhythm patterns are similarly unprotectable â€” basic timing structures that define musical genres.

**But**: The Ninth Circuit has held that a *collection of otherwise unprotectable elements* may be eligible for copyright protection if the selection and arrangement is sufficiently original.

Our claim: The specific combinations of motifs into melodic chains, filtered by musically-informed rules, scored by documented aesthetic criteria, and curated by human judgment, constitute original creative authorship.

### The Evidence Trail

The copyright claim is strengthened by documenting:

1. **Motif selection** â€” Why these 23 motifs out of hundreds of possible interval pairs?
2. **Filter design** â€” Why 14 semitones? Why arch contours? These encode aesthetic preferences.
3. **Scoring weights** â€” The choice to weight harmonic clarity at 0.25 and leap resolution at 0.15 is a creative judgment.
4. **Curation records** â€” Every up/down vote, every rejection reason, timestamped.
5. **Album arrangement** â€” Sequencing melodies into coherent aesthetic arcs.

The pipeline code itself is a creative artifact documenting the selection methodology.

### What the Case Law Says

From our research on music copyright (see transcript for full citations):

- **Rhythm alone is generally unprotectable** â€” *Swirsky v. Carey* lists "key, tempo, rhythm, and genre" as unprotected elements
- **Combinations can be protected** â€” "A substantial similarity can be found in a combination of elements, even if those elements are individually unprotected"
- **Melody is primary** â€” Courts focus heavily on melody as offering "the greatest potential for originality"
- **The "total concept and feel" test** â€” Lay observers judge substantial similarity holistically
- **Filtration is required** â€” Courts must filter out unprotectable elements before comparing works

The *Gray v. Hudson* standard is particularly relevant: an 8-note ostinato made of common elements gets only "thin" protection, requiring "virtual identity" to infringe. This cuts both ways â€” our melodies may get thin protection, but they're also unlikely to infringe existing works built from the same building blocks.

**Rhythm patterns** (from RHYTHMS_ALLOWED.md):
- 63 two-bar patterns (repeating to form 4-bar phrases)
- Constrained by: beat 1 attack, resolution rules, pickup restrictions
- All are scÃ¨nes Ã  faire â€” basic timing structures

### Generation Model

We explored two models:

1. **Note overlap**: Motifs share boundary notes. Any motif can follow any other. 4 motifs = 9 notes.

2. **Interval overlap**: Motifs share boundary intervals. The last interval of motif N must equal the first interval of motif N+1. Much more constrained (avg branching factor ~2.7), but every 3-note sliding window is a recognized motif.

The interval overlap model is legally stronger â€” the entire melody is "always in vocabulary."

### Filtering Pipeline

1. **Range constraint** â€” Cumulative pitch stays within Â±14 semitones
2. **No immediate repetition** â€” Same motif can't appear consecutively
3. **Contour coherence** â€” Prefer arch/valley shapes, reject zigzag
4. **Junction quality** â€” Connection intervals must be stepwise or triadic

### Scoring Dimensions

Each chain is scored 0â€“1 on:
- Harmonic clarity (fraction of windows fitting recognized chords)
- Resolution quality (final note stability in detected key)
- Climax placement (peak in golden zone 50â€“80%)
- Intervallic variety (Shannon entropy of intervals)
- Leap resolution (leaps followed by contrary-motion steps)
- Key stability (Krumhansl-Kessler correlation)

Weighted composite score determines review priority.
